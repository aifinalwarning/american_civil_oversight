The committee stage is often the most influential time for citizen input. Most bills die in committee, so contacting your representatives before committee votes can have significant impact.

Types of Legislation

  • Bills (H.R. / S.) — The most common form of legislation. H.R. bills originate in the House, S. bills in the Senate.
  • Joint Resolutions (H.J.Res. / S.J.Res.) — Similar to bills but often used for constitutional amendments or continuing appropriations.
  • Concurrent Resolutions (H.Con.Res. / S.Con.Res.) — Used for matters affecting both chambers, like setting the budget. Not signed by the President.
  • Simple Resolutions (H.Res. / S.Res.) — Address matters of one chamber only, like internal rules.

Executive Actions

The President can take several types of executive actions that don't require Congressional approval. These can have significant policy impact.

Types of Executive Actions

Executive Orders

Directives from the President to federal agencies on how to implement or interpret existing laws. They have the force of law but can be overturned by subsequent presidents or by Congress.

Presidential Memoranda

Similar to executive orders but typically more limited in scope. They direct specific agencies on particular matters.

Proclamations

Official announcements, often ceremonial (like declaring holidays) but sometimes with legal effect (like trade proclamations).

Why We Track These

Executive actions can significantly affect policy without the deliberation of the legislative process. Tracking them helps citizens stay informed about changes that might affect their rights and interests.

Agency Rulemaking

Federal agencies create detailed rules to implement laws passed by Congress. This "regulatory" process often has more direct impact on daily life than the laws themselves.

The Rulemaking Process

1
Proposed Rule

Agency publishes proposed rule in Federal Register

2
Comment Period

Public submits comments (30-90 days typical)

3
Review

Agency reviews and responds to comments

4
Final Rule

Agency publishes final rule with effective date

Your Comments Matter

Agencies are legally required to consider substantive public comments. Well-reasoned comments citing specific facts or personal experiences can influence final rules. This is one of the most direct ways citizens can participate in federal policy.

How to Submit Effective Comments

  1. Read the proposed rule — Understand what's being proposed before commenting.
  2. Be specific — Reference specific sections of the rule you support or oppose.
  3. Provide evidence — Use facts, data, or personal experiences to support your position.
  4. Suggest alternatives — If you oppose something, propose a better approach.
  5. Submit early — Don't wait until the deadline; earlier comments may receive more attention.

Risk Scoring Methodology

Our risk scoring system is designed to be fully transparent. We believe citizens should understand exactly how we evaluate legislation so they can adjust their own assessments accordingly.

Important Note

Risk scores are not value judgments about whether legislation is "good" or "bad." They indicate potential significance and urgency for public attention. A high-risk environmental bill could be one that helps OR harms the environment—the score reflects impact magnitude, not direction.

Domain Categories

We classify legislation impact across four primary domains, weighted by their prioritization in user settings:

Environmental & Climate (35% weight)

Legislation affecting air quality, water resources, land use, wildlife, climate policy, energy production, and environmental regulations. Keywords: climate, EPA, pollution, emissions, conservation, renewable, carbon, ecosystem.

Healthcare & Public Safety (28% weight)

Legislation affecting healthcare access, medical research, drug policy, public health programs, and patient rights. Keywords: health, Medicare, Medicaid, FDA, pharmaceutical, insurance, vaccine, disease.

Civil Liberties & Rights (22% weight)

Legislation affecting constitutional rights, privacy, voting access, due process, freedom of speech, and equality protections. Keywords: rights, freedom, privacy, surveillance, constitutional, voting, discrimination.

Economic & Labor (15% weight)

Legislation affecting taxes, federal spending, trade policy, labor protections, wages, and financial regulation. Keywords: tax, budget, trade, tariff, labor, wage, employment, banking.
